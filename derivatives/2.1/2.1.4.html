2.1.4 Power, Product, & Quotient Rules

Introduction

Now that we’ve gotten the complicated stuff (*wink wink*) out of the way, we can finally move on to the shortcut ways to find a derivative instead of having to use that contrived limit definition. The most basic rule applies to all $x^n$ terms and is called the power rule:

$y = ax^n \implies y' = anx^{n - 1}$

You can apply this to as many added or subtracted terms as you want, as long as they are in the form $x^n$ (yes, the exponent can be negative or fractional too). And if you need to take a derivative of a constant, the derivative of a constant is always 0.

However, once terms involving a variable are multiplied or divided with other non-constant terms, we need to first invoke the product rule and the quotient rule:

Conversely, the two conditions for differentiability are that the graph is continuous and that its derivative (or limit definition of a derivative) exists anywhere (at all points). In addition, all polynomials and constants are differentiable and continuous everywhere.

Example Problems

1. Evaluate the differentiability of the piecewise function $\\ f(x) = \begin{cases}x + 4, & x \le 1\\ \frac{x^3}{2x^2}, & 1 \lt x \le 3\\ \frac{1}{18}, & x > 3\\ \end{cases}\\ $

We can logically deduct the continuity and differentiability of each part of the function (yes, calculus is a lot of work):

Part Continuous? Differentiable?
$x + 4$ Yes (polynomial) Yes (polynomial)
At $x = 1$ No ($\lim_{x \to 1^-}{(x + 4)} \neq \lim_{x \to 1^+}{\frac{1}{2x^2}}$ as learnt from continuity proofs) No (not continuous)
$\frac{x^3}{2x^2}$ Yes (only discontinuity at x = 0 is not in its domain) Yes ($\lim_{h \to 0}{\frac{\frac{(x + h)^3}{2(x + h)^2} - \frac{x^3}{2x^2}}{h}}$ exists everywhere between x = 1 and x = 3)
At $x = 3$ Yes ($\lim_{x \to 3^-}{\frac{1}{2x^2}} = \lim_{x \to 3^+}{\frac{1}{18}}$) No ($\lim_{h \to 0^-}{\frac{\frac{1}{2(3 + h)^2} - \frac{1}{2(3)^2}}{h}} \neq \lim_{h \to 0^+}{\frac{\frac{1}{18} - \frac{1}{18}}{h}}$)
$\frac{1}{18}$ Yes (constant) Yes (constant)

Therefore, we can arrive at the conclusion that this piecewise function is not differentiable at either of its boundary points and differentiable everywhere else (i.e. differentiable on $(-\infty,1) \cup (1,3) \cup (3,\infty)$).

Practice Problems

1. Is the function $y = 8x^4 + x^3 + \frac{1}{2}x - 12$ differentiable everywhere?

Answer (hover): yes (polynomial)

2. Is the function $y = \frac{9}{x - 4.5}$ differentiable on the interval $(0, 10)$?

Answer (hover): no (not continuous at x = 4.5)

3. Where (if any) is the piecewise function $\\ f(x) = \begin{cases}\frac{1}{2}x^2, & x \le 2\\ 2x - 2, & x > 2\\ \end{cases}\\ $ not differentiable?

Answer (hover): Differentiable everywhere (polynomials & limit definition of derivative at $x = 2$ exists)

4. On which interval(s) is/are the piecewise function $\\ f(x) = \begin{cases}3x^3, & x \le 0\\ 0, & 0 \lt x \le 4\\ x - 4, & x > 4\\ \end{cases}\\ $ differentiable?

Answer (hover): $(-\infty,4) \cup (4, \infty)$ (polynomials but limit definition of derivative DNE at $x = 4$, shows on graph as a corner)
