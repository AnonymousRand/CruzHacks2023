2.1.6 Derivative Identities

Introduction

You may have noticed that I included an example in the previous lessons with a $\sin$ and a $\ln$ function in it, and if you have any hope of making it through calculus at all, you should’ve wondered to yourself how those derivatives are calculated, and proceeded to go down a rabbit hole for the next three hours. Kidding—the only other way you can get through calculus is with this guide. Anyways, in case you haven’t already discovered the secrets of the universe, I’ll be going over the most exciting part of math—memorizing formulas.

As if we haven’t been doing that for the past 4 lessons.

Function Derivative
$\sin{x}$ $\cos{x}$
$\cos{x}$ $-\sin{x}$
$\tan{x}$ $\sec^2{x}$
$\sec{x}$ $\sec{x}\tan{x}$

$\ln{x}$ $\frac{1}{x}$

$a^x$ $a^x\ln{a}$

Yep, that’s a lot.

No, there’s no shortcut. You just have to do a lot of practice.

Ok ok, maybe there is a shortcut. Any trig and inverse trig function whose name starts with a ‘c’ has a negative derivative, otherwise it’s positive. $\sin \& \cos$, $\csc \& \sec$, and $\tan \& \cot$ share some similarities.

But that’s about it.

And also, don’t forget the chain rule.

Example Problems

1. Find the derivative of $y = \csc(\ln(x^2))$.

This is a double chain rule because there are 3 functions: $f(x) = \csc(x),\ g(x) = \ln(x), \text{ and } h(x) = x^2$. $\csc(x)$ is extra tricky too due to the fact that its derivative has 2 parts, and both parts must include the unchanged $g(h(x))$ inside before we can get to $g'(h(x))$.

2. Find the derivative of $y = \sin^2{\left(\log{\left(x^{e} \right)} \right)}cos{\left(\frac{x}{2} \right)}$

I am not sane, so this is a triple chain rule. With a product rule.

To start, note that $\sin^2{x}$ holds the same meaning as $(\sin{x})^2$—the exponent is the outermost function. The next function is $\sin{x}$, followed by $\log{x}$ (a default base 10 logarithm) and $x^e$, a power rule (different from $e^x$!).

To solve this, we invoke the product rule, since it is the overall, encapsulating operation, and perform this triple chain rule when it is time to differentiate the $\sin^2{(\log{\left(x^e \right)})}$ part. Also don’t forget the single chain rule with $\cos{\left(\frac{x}{2} \right)}$.

*Deep inhale*

-

$y' = -\frac{1}{2}\sin^2{(\log{\left(x^e \right)})}\sin{\left(\frac{x}{2} \right)} + 2\cos{\left(\frac{x}{2} \right)}\sin{(\log{\left(x^e \right)})}\cos{(\log{\left(x^e \right)})}\left(\frac{1}{x^e\ln{10}} \right)\left(ex^{e - 1} \right)$

Practice Problems

1. Find the derivative of $y = \sin{(\cos{(\tan{(x)})})}$.

Answer (hover): $y' = \cos{(\sin{(x)})}\cos{(x)}$ (you don’t actually need to do a double chain rule here; you can use your trig identities to realize that $\cos{(tan{(x)})} = \cos{\left(\frac{\sin{(x)}}{cos{(x)}}\right)} = \sin{(x)}$ and therefore the entire function can be simplified to $y = \sin{(\sin({x}))}$ which only requires 1 chain rule)

2. Find the derivative of $y = \sec^{-1}{\left(\ln{\left(x^3\right)}\right)}$.

Answer (hover): $y' = \frac{\frac{3x^2}{x^3}}{|\ln{\left(x^3\right)}|\sqrt{\left(\ln{\left(x^3\right)^2 + 1}\right)}}$ (unsimplified)

3. Find the derivative of $y = 4^{3x^5} \cdot x$

Answer (hover): $y' = 4^{3x^5}\ln{4} \cdot 15x^4 \cdot x + 4^{3x^5}(1)$ (product rule + chain rule)
